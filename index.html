Notes & Gracenotes · Introduction

The GHB Scale

The Great Highland Bagpipe (GHB) scale has 9 notes spanning from Low G to High A, placed on 5 lines called a staff or stave.


During this tutorial we will cover each note individually so we can learn its position on the staff.


This tutorial is designed to be used alongside the guidance of a competent bagpipe tutor who can direct you on how to play these notes on your practise chanter. The guidance of a tutor will be essential to complete the more advanced levels of this tutorial.


Click Next to learn the first note.

Low G

Low G is the lowest pitched note, positioned on the 2nd line up from the bottom of the staff.

Notes & Gracenotes · Note 9 of 9

High A

High A is a little different from the other notes — it sits above the staff with no line to rest on. Instead, a short line called a ledger line is placed through the note head to imitate a 6th line.


Because of this unique feature, High A is the easiest note to identify on the staff!

Gracenotes · Introduction

Welcome to Gracenotes

A gracenote is one of the first embellishments or movements you will learn. It is simply played by lifting the corresponding finger and returning it sharply to the chanter. A gracenote should make a quick chirp sound. Consult your tutor for further guidance.


As you will see, it is written by placing a small note head at the desired pitch, with the note stem travelling upward with 3 flags placed on the stem.


We will now cover the common gracenotes.

Gracenotes · High G Gracenote

High G Gracenote

Commonly called just the "G Gracenote". It is written with the small note head placed in the same position as the standard High G note — in the space above the top line.


Be aware the gracenote note head is considerably smaller than a main note. All gracenotes are written just before the note they are played on. For the purpose of this tutorial all gracenotes are shown before a Low A.

𝄞
Gracenotes · E Gracenote

E Gracenote

The E Gracenote is written with the small note head placed in the same position as the standard E note — in the 4th space up from the bottom of the staff, between the 4th and 5th lines.


As with all gracenotes, it is written just before the note it is played on. Here it is shown before a Low A.

𝄞
Gracenotes · D Gracenote

D Gracenote

The D Gracenote is written with the small note head placed in the same position as the standard D note — on the 4th line from the bottom of the staff.


As with all gracenotes, it is written just before the note it is played on. Here it is shown before a Low A.

𝄞

Beginner Embellishments · Strikes

Strikes

Strikes are used as a means of separating two notes of the same pitch. They are made by striking the chanter with one, two or three fingers simultaneously in order to momentarily cover the holes necessary for the sounding of the note which appears as a gracenote between them.


Your tutor will be able to give further guidance.

Strikes · Low A Strike

Low A Strike

The Low A Strike consists of two main Low A notes with a Low G gracenote between them.


To play a Low A strike we simply strike the chanter rapidly to Low G with the Low A finger.


Consult your tutor for guidance on how to play this embellishment on the practise chanter.

𝄞
Strikes · B Strike

B Strike

The B Strike has two variants:


Open B Strike — uses a Low A gracenote in the middle, closing briefly to Low A for a more open sound.


Closed B Strike — uses a Low G gracenote in the middle, closing down to Low G for a slightly darker, more closed sound. Your tutor will guide you on when to use each variant.

Strikes · D Strike

D Strike

The D Strike also has two variants:


Open D Strike — uses a C gracenote in the middle, closing briefly to C for a brighter, more open sound.


Closed D Strike — uses a Low G gracenote in the middle, closing all the way down to Low G for a heavier, more closed sound. Your tutor will guide you on when to use each variant.

Beginner Embellishments · Tachums

Tachums

A Tachum is an embellishment used to move between two notes of different pitch. It consists of a gracenote before each main note, creating a smooth decorative movement between them.


There are two common Tachums — B to Low G and C to Low A. Each uses a G gracenote before the first note and a D gracenote before the second.


Your tutor will be able to give further guidance on how to play these.

Tachums · B to Low G

B to Low G Tachum

This Tachum moves from B down to Low G. A G gracenote is placed before the main B note, followed by a D gracenote before the main Low G note.


Consult your tutor for guidance on how to play this on the practise chanter.

𝄞
Tachums · C to Low A

C to Low A Tachum

This Tachum moves from C down to Low A. A G gracenote is placed before the main C note, followed by a D gracenote before the main Low A note.


Consult your tutor for guidance on how to play this on the practise chanter.

𝄞
Tachums · Complete

🎉 Well Done!

You have now covered both Tachums and Closed Tachums in the beginner embellishments level.


Repetition and practise with your tutor will be essential to mastering these on the chanter.

Tachums · Closed Tachum

Closed Tachum

A Closed Tachum is virtually the same as the ones you have just learnt with a slight difference in the note values and lengths.


Tachums are commonly played with the first note cut/shorter and the second note held/longer. This is displayed in the notation by a short bar at the bottom of the stem for the cut note, and a dot after the second held note.


When played the movement sounds snappy and rigid.


Your tutor will be able to elaborate further on cut and held notes as these will appear in many aspects of GHB music.

Advanced Embellishments · Introduction

Advanced Embellishments

In this section we cover many of the most important advanced embellishments used in Great Highland Bagpipe music.


Each embellishment is shown with a staff diagram. Your tutor will guide you on how to play these on the practice chanter.


Click Next to begin.

Advanced Embellishments · 1 of 4

Taorluath

The Taorluath is one of the most recognised embellishments in pipe music. It consists of two Low G gracenotes separated by a D gracenote followed by an E gracenote.


A Taorluath is virtually a Grip with an E gracenote at the end. The E gracenote is played exactly on the destination note which will follow the embellishment.


Your tutor will guide you on how to play this.

𝄞
Advanced Embellishments · 2 of 4

Grip

A Grip consists of two Low G's separated by a D gracenote. The two short Low G's should be of equal in duration. You should be able to hear the two Low G's clearly when played correctly.


Your tutor will guide you further on this.

𝄞
Advanced Embellishments · 3 of 4

D Throw

The D Throw can be played from any note but always is followed by a D. Consult your tutor on how to deliver this movement correctly as there are variances in D Throw interpretation.

𝄞
Advanced Embellishments · 4 of 4

Birl

The Birl is used on Low A and consists of a Low G, Low A and another Low G gracenote between two main Low A notes. It gives a flowing, rolling sound.


Your tutor will guide you on how to play this on the practice chanter.

𝄞
Advanced Embellishments · Complete

🎉 Well Done!

You have now covered many of the main advanced embellishments — Taorluath, Grip, D Throw and Birl.


Repetition and practice with your tutor will be essential to mastering these on the chanter.

Doublings · Introduction

Doublings

Doublings are exactly what the word implies — the note is doubled, but it is done rapidly and precedes the sounding of the note proper.


Your tutor will be able to give further guidance on how to play these on the practise chanter.


Click Next to learn the first doubling.

Doublings · Low G Doubling

Low G Doubling

A Low G Doubling is simply a G gracenote on Low G followed rapidly by a D gracenote.


Note: If travelling to Low G from another note, the G gracenote of the doubling is played exactly on the Low G note during the transition, then the D gracenote will follow when on Low G.


Your tutor will give you further guidance on this.

𝄞
Doublings · Low A Doubling

Low A Doubling

A Low A Doubling is simply a G gracenote on Low A followed rapidly by a D gracenote.


Note: If travelling to Low A from another note, the G gracenote of the doubling is played exactly on the Low A note during the transition, then the D gracenote will follow when on Low A.


Your tutor will give you further guidance on this.

𝄞
Doublings · B Doubling

B Doubling

A B Doubling works in the same way as the Low A Doubling — a G gracenote on B followed rapidly by a D gracenote.


The same principle applies when travelling to B from another note. Your tutor will give you further guidance.

𝄞
Doublings · C Doubling

C Doubling

A C Doubling works in the same way — a G gracenote on C followed rapidly by a D gracenote.


The same principle applies when travelling to C from another note. Your tutor will give you further guidance.

𝄞
Doublings · D Doubling

D Doubling

A D Doubling differs from Low A, B and C doublings. It uses a G gracenote followed rapidly by an E gracenote.

𝄞
Doublings · E Doubling

E Doubling

An E Doubling uses a G gracenote followed rapidly by an F gracenote.

𝄞
Doublings · F Doubling

F Doubling

An F Doubling uses a High G gracenote followed rapidly by another High G gracenote — both at the same pitch.

𝄞
Doublings · High G Doubling

High G Doubling

The High G Doubling is made in a similar manner to the High A Doubling, using the High G finger for the quick touch. It is shown here preceded by a Low A.

𝄞
Doublings · High A Doubling

High A Doubling

The High "A" doubling is made by sounding the High "A" first, then as quickly as possible touching it again with the thumb or High "A" finger. The High "G" is made in similar manner but, of course, using the High "G" finger for the quick touch. It is illustrated here from Low A.

Doublings · From High G

Doublings from High G

When playing certain tunes the scenario may arise where a doubling is required when coming from High G. When on High G the G finger is off the chanter so it can't be used for the doubling. To resolve this we substitute the G gracenote of the doubling with a High A or thumb gracenote.


I have displayed this below as a Low A Doubling from High G. This G gracenote substitution is used across all doublings.

𝄞
Doublings · D Doubling from High G

D Doubling from High G

Here is an example of a D Doubling played from High G. As seen, the G gracenote is substituted with the thumb gracenote just like the previous Low A Doubling. This is the case across all other doublings below High G.

𝄞
Doublings · Half Doublings

Half Doublings

When attempting to play a doubling from High A we find that the High G finger is unavailable. Therefore we can only play a half doubling.


To do this we simply close to the destination note and sound the 2nd gracenote of the doubling. The example below shows a half doubling to Low A where we close to the Low A from High A and immediately play a D gracenote on Low A. This completes the Half Doubling on Low A.
